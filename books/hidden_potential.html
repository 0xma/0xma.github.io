Ten Lessons from Hidden Potential: The Science of Achieving Greater Things by Adam Grant

1. Talent Isn't Fixed, Potential Is Expandable

We often believe that talent is something people are born with, a fixed trait that defines their abilities from an early age. This notion causes people to limit their own potential, thinking that if they don't have a natural aptitude for something, they cannot excel at it. However, Adam Grant challenges this idea by emphasizing that talent is only a starting point, not the end destination. What matters more than talent is how much effort someone puts into developing their skills.

Grant argues that potential can be cultivated through hard work, dedication, and continuous learning. Rather than accepting limitations, people should embrace the mindset that they can improve over time. The more effort and intention they put into honing a particular skill, the more their potential expands. By focusing on consistent improvement, individuals can achieve far beyond what they or others initially thought possible.

The belief that potential is expandable shifts the focus from a fixed, innate capacity to a growth-oriented mindset. This helps people overcome the barriers of self-doubt and societal expectations. Instead of wondering whether they are talented enough, they should ask themselves how much they are willing to learn and how far they are prepared to go. In doing so, they unlock the capacity for greater achievements.


2. Grit Can Be Misleading

Grit is often celebrated as the key to success, but Grant warns that it has limitations. While perseverance is important, blind persistence can be counterproductive if it leads someone down a path that’s no longer working or sustainable. Grit can sometimes make people cling too tightly to a particular goal, even when it might be time to pivot or explore different strategies. The romanticization of grit can make individuals feel that if they quit, they’re failing, when in fact, flexibility may be the smarter choice.

Instead of glorifying never giving up, Grant encourages people to adopt a more nuanced view of grit. He explains that perseverance should be paired with reflection. People need to recognize when it's appropriate to change direction or explore new interests. This doesn’t mean giving up easily but being strategic about when to persist and when to let go of goals that are no longer aligned with their growth or well-being.

By recognizing the limits of grit, individuals can avoid burnout and take a more balanced approach to long-term success. Flexibility and adaptability can actually enhance persistence by ensuring that people are pursuing goals that truly resonate with their evolving interests and values. In short, persistence is important, but it should be driven by informed choices rather than an unyielding attachment to a single path.


3. The Power of Deliberate Practice

Repetition alone doesn’t lead to mastery. Grant highlights that what sets apart top performers is their commitment to deliberate practice. Unlike routine practice, which involves doing the same thing over and over, deliberate practice focuses on improving specific weaknesses, receiving targeted feedback, and pushing beyond the current limits. This methodical and strategic approach to skill-building is what enables individuals to achieve exceptional results.

Deliberate practice requires a mindset that’s open to criticism and learning. It’s about being intentional with one’s efforts and seeking ways to stretch abilities. This might mean focusing on the most challenging aspects of a task or skill, which can be uncomfortable but ultimately more rewarding. It also involves breaking down large goals into smaller, more manageable steps, so that progress becomes tangible over time.

The concept of deliberate practice illustrates that natural talent can only take you so far. By focusing on where you need to improve and continuously refining your skills, you increase the likelihood of success. This approach moves away from simply “working hard” and towards working smart, ensuring that every effort made contributes meaningfully to your growth.


4. Open-mindedness and Adaptability

One of the biggest barriers to growth is a closed mind, one that’s rigid and unwilling to change. Grant stresses the importance of open-mindedness, encouraging people to welcome new ideas, challenge their own assumptions, and remain flexible in their beliefs. This mindset is crucial for expanding potential because it allows individuals to see possibilities they might have otherwise ignored or dismissed. Being open to new information allows people to pivot when needed, leading to more effective problem-solving and innovation.

Adaptability is closely linked to open-mindedness. In a rapidly changing world, the ability to adjust to new circumstances or rethink old approaches can make the difference between success and stagnation. Grant suggests that people who cling too tightly to old ways of thinking limit their ability to learn and grow. Those who can embrace change are better positioned to seize opportunities, adapt to challenges, and continue their development over time.

The willingness to change your mind in the face of new evidence is a hallmark of a growth mindset. Instead of viewing this flexibility as a sign of weakness or indecision, Grant argues that it’s a strength. Those who are able to adapt are less likely to get stuck in their ways and more likely to find creative solutions to complex problems. They see setbacks as learning experiences and embrace uncertainty as a space for growth.


5. Focus on Your Zone of Proximal Development

The concept of the zone of proximal development (ZPD) originates from educational psychology and refers to tasks that are slightly beyond one’s current capabilities but within reach if given the right support. Grant encourages people to operate in this zone, where challenges are neither too easy nor overwhelming, because this is where the most meaningful growth occurs. When people consistently stretch themselves, they develop new skills, knowledge, and resilience.

The key to operating within your ZPD is finding tasks that challenge you just enough to push you out of your comfort zone, but not so much that you become discouraged. It’s about seeking gradual improvement and celebrating small wins along the way. Grant suggests that aiming for incremental progress helps people stay motivated while continuing to grow at a sustainable pace.

Grant also emphasizes the importance of mentorship and support when working in your ZPD. Having a coach, mentor, or peer who can provide guidance or feedback helps individuals stay on track and avoid frustration. With the right support, people are able to tackle challenges that might have seemed impossible on their own, accelerating their development and potential.


6. The Myth of the Lone Genius

Contrary to popular belief, most breakthroughs don’t come from lone geniuses working in isolation. Grant dismantles the myth of the self-made success, showing that collaboration and social support are often essential components of high achievement. Great minds do not operate in a vacuum; they rely on networks of colleagues, mentors, and peers who offer new perspectives, feedback, and opportunities for growth.

One of the biggest advantages of working with others is the diversity of ideas that collaboration brings. When people from different backgrounds or with different skill sets come together, they create a richer pool of knowledge. This exchange of ideas often leads to innovation and helps individuals see challenges from new angles, ultimately leading to better outcomes than what could be achieved alone.

Grant also emphasizes the value of mentorship in helping individuals reach their full potential. Having a mentor provides guidance, inspiration, and a sense of accountability. By learning from others who have walked the path before, individuals can avoid common pitfalls and accelerate their own progress. Ultimately, success is not about going it alone, but about building and leveraging a strong support network.


7. The Benefits of Rejection and Failure

Rejection and failure are inevitable on the path to success, but rather than viewing them as setbacks, Grant encourages people to see them as opportunities for growth. Every rejection or failure provides valuable lessons about what works and what doesn’t. By learning from these experiences, individuals can refine their approach, improve their skills, and become more resilient.

Grant highlights that some of the most successful people in history faced repeated failures before achieving their breakthroughs. What sets them apart is not the absence of failure, but their ability to bounce back and persist in the face of adversity. They view failure not as an end but as a necessary part of the journey towards success.

Moreover, rejection can provide clarity and motivation. Being turned away or failing at something forces people to reevaluate their goals, methods, or attitudes. This reflection can lead to important changes that ultimately bring them closer to achieving their potential. Embracing failure as a learning tool helps people remain resilient and focused on long-term growth.


8. Growth Comes from Diverse Experiences

Sticking to familiar environments and routines can limit your growth. Grant emphasizes that diverse experiences—whether they involve meeting new people, exploring new ideas, or immersing yourself in different cultures—broaden your perspective and spark creativity. Exposure to diverse environments helps people see the world through new lenses, leading to innovative solutions and personal growth.

Grant argues that by stepping outside your comfort zone, you expose yourself to new challenges and opportunities. These diverse experiences allow you to gather new information, question assumptions, and adapt to different contexts. Whether it's learning a new skill, traveling, or engaging with people who have different viewpoints, diversity fosters learning and strengthens problem-solving abilities.

Incorporating diversity into your life not only enhances creativity but also prepares you to face complex, real-world challenges. Grant suggests that surrounding yourself with different perspectives makes you more open to change and better equipped to navigate uncertainty. This openness is crucial for unlocking potential, as it leads to more flexible thinking and a broader understanding of the world.


9. Learning Over Achievement

Grant challenges the achievement-oriented mindset that dominates many aspects of modern life, from education to professional success. He argues that focusing solely on outcomes—such as grades, promotions, or awards—can be limiting because it often comes at the expense of long-term growth. Instead, people should prioritize learning and improvement, which leads to deeper fulfillment and sustainable success.

When people prioritize learning, they shift their focus from short-term results to long-term development. This mindset encourages curiosity, experimentation, and a willingness to take risks, which are crucial for personal growth. Grant points out that those who value learning over achievement are more resilient, as they’re less likely to be discouraged by setbacks and more motivated by the desire to improve.

By adopting a learning mindset, individuals become more adaptable and open to change. They’re willing to step outside their comfort zones and embrace challenges as opportunities for growth. This approach leads to more meaningful success because it’s rooted in continuous development, rather than the pursuit of external validation.


10. Potential Is a Marathon, Not a Sprint

The journey to realizing your potential is not a quick one. Grant emphasizes that success is a marathon, not a sprint. This means that long-term growth requires sustained effort, patience, and a willingness to play the long game. People often expect instant results, but true potential is built over time, through consistent and dedicated work.

Grant encourages readers to embrace long-term thinking, rather than focusing on immediate rewards or quick wins. This perspective helps people avoid burnout and frustration when progress is slow. It also allows individuals to develop the resilience needed to overcome setbacks, as they understand that the path to success is not always linear.

By viewing potential as something that develops over a lifetime, individuals can maintain a sense of purpose and motivation even when facing obstacles. This approach fosters perseverance and encourages people to keep pushing forward, knowing that every step brings them closer to their long-term goals. In the end, it’s the steady accumulation of effort over time that leads to the most profound achievements.
